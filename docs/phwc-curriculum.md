## PHWC Coaching Foundations
<!-- source_url: https://www.nutritionaltherapy.com/health-coach -->

### The Five Coaching Foundations — Overview

The PHWC program is built on five foundational principles that guide every aspect of the coaching relationship: Client-Centered, Bio-Individuality, Trust, Resilience, and Ethics. These five coaching foundations parallel the NTP program's Five Foundations of Health but operate in a different domain — while the NTP foundations are physiological, the PHWC foundations are relational and professional. Together, these principles define what makes a PHWC-trained coach distinct from other coaching approaches. They ensure that the coaching relationship always centers the client's autonomy, honors their uniqueness, creates safety for vulnerability, builds capacity for sustained change, and maintains professional integrity.

### Foundation — Client-Centered: The Client Is the Expert

The client-centered foundation is the philosophical core of the PHWC approach. It holds that the client is the expert in their own life — not the coach. This principle fundamentally distinguishes the PHWC from the NTP, where the practitioner acts as the nutrition expert. In PHWC practice, the coach's role is to strengthen the client's self-confidence and belief in their capacity for progress. Rather than prescribing solutions or directing outcomes, the coach facilitates a process through which the client discovers their own insights, sets their own goals, and designs their own action steps. The coach provides the framework, the questions, and the supportive presence — but the client provides the answers. This approach builds lasting self-efficacy because clients learn to trust their own judgment rather than depending on an external expert.

### Foundation — Bio-Individuality: No One-Size-Fits-All Coaching

While bio-individuality in the NTP context refers to biological uniqueness in nutrition, in the PHWC context it extends to the recognition that there is no one-size-fits-all approach to health coaching. Each client brings a unique combination of personality, values, readiness for change, life circumstances, cultural background, support systems, and barriers. PHWC coaches are trained to tailor their coaching methods to meet each individual client where they are — adjusting communication style, pacing, challenge level, and approach based on what serves each particular person. A technique that catalyzes breakthrough for one client may feel threatening to another. The bio-individual coaching approach requires practitioners to be flexible, observant, and responsive rather than following a rigid coaching script.

### Foundation — Trust: Safe Space, Active Listening, Compassion

Trust is the foundation that makes the coaching relationship possible. PHWCs are trained to create safe spaces where clients feel seen, heard, encouraged, trusted, and supported. This requires mastery of active listening — the ability to be fully present with a client without judgment, agenda, or the urge to fix. Compassion is the emotional dimension of trust: the coach meets the client's struggles, setbacks, and vulnerabilities with genuine care rather than clinical detachment. Trust is not assumed or declared — it is built through consistent demonstration of reliability, confidentiality, non-judgment, and genuine interest in the client's wellbeing. Without trust, clients will not share the honest information that makes effective coaching possible.

### Foundation — Resilience: Growth Mindset, Self-Compassion

The resilience foundation addresses the reality that behavior change is not linear. Clients will face ambivalence, setbacks, and periods of frustration throughout their wellness journey. PHWCs are trained to foster growth mindsets — the belief that abilities and habits can be developed through effort and persistence rather than being fixed traits. When clients stumble, coaches encourage self-compassion rather than self-criticism, helping clients reframe setbacks as learning opportunities rather than failures. This foundation also teaches coaches to support clients in developing their own resilience capacity, building internal resources and coping strategies that persist long after the coaching relationship ends. The goal is a client who can navigate future challenges independently.

### Foundation — Ethics: Professional Boundaries, Self-Care

The ethics foundation establishes the professional boundaries that protect both coach and client. PHWCs are trained to maintain strict scope limitations, never overstepping into territory that belongs to licensed professionals, medical providers, or nutrition experts like NTPs. The ethics foundation also emphasizes the coach's responsibility to prioritize their own health and wellness — a coach who is burnt out, unhealthy, or unwell cannot effectively support client transformation. Professional conduct standards cover confidentiality, informed consent, dual relationships, referral protocols, and the ethical obligation to discontinue coaching when a client's needs exceed the PHWC scope. Ethics is not treated as a set of restrictions but as the framework that enables trust and makes the coaching relationship professionally sustainable.

## PHWC Curriculum — Month by Month
<!-- source_url: https://www.nutritionaltherapy.com/health-coach -->

### Month 1 — Foundations of Coaching

Month 1 establishes the fundamental skills and philosophy of the PHWC approach. Students are introduced to coaching as a distinct professional discipline, learning how it differs from therapy, consulting, mentoring, and teaching. The curriculum covers the client-centered relationship, emphasizing how to establish trust and rapport from the first interaction. Active listening and presence are taught as foundational communication skills — the ability to listen without planning a response, to notice what is said and unsaid, and to remain fully engaged with the client's experience. Students also learn to navigate client emotions and energy, understanding how emotional states affect readiness for change and how to hold space for difficult feelings without trying to fix or resolve them.

### Month 2 — Expanding Coaching Skills

Month 2 builds on the foundational skills with more nuanced coaching techniques. Students learn to expand the conversation beyond surface-level topics, using open-ended questions and powerful inquiries to help clients explore deeper motivations and values. Reflections — the practice of mirroring back what a client has said in a way that deepens their understanding — are studied as a core coaching technique. Coach preparation prior to sessions is covered, including how to review previous session notes, set intentions, and create a focused agenda. The module culminates in goals and implementing action, where students learn to support clients in translating insights into concrete, actionable steps while ensuring that goals remain self-determined by the client.

### Month 3 — Applied Practice

Month 3 transitions from skill development to hands-on application with real clients. Students learn client intake form design and administration, the initial coaching session structure, and follow-up session formats. The first two coaching mentor sessions occur during this month, providing students with supervised practice and direct feedback from experienced coaches. Students practice conducting full coaching sessions from intake through follow-up, learning to maintain the flow of a coaching conversation while staying client-centered. The focus and refocus skill is introduced — the ability to gently guide a wandering conversation back to the client's stated goals without being directive or controlling.

### Month 4 — Deepening Client Work

Month 4 explores the more advanced dimensions of the coaching relationship. Client freedom of choice and intrinsic motivation are studied — understanding why externally imposed goals fail and how to cultivate the client's own internal drive for change. Students learn to facilitate client awareness, perspective shifts, and insights — the breakthrough moments where a client sees their situation differently. Client self-efficacy and improved support systems are addressed, teaching coaches how to help clients build confidence in their ability to succeed and how to leverage existing support networks. Various coaching models are introduced, providing students with different frameworks they can draw upon depending on the client's needs. The third coaching mentor session occurs during this month.

### Month 5 — Advanced Client Sessions

Month 5 focuses on the later stages of the coaching relationship and advanced techniques. Client active experimentation and self-discovery are covered — supporting clients as they test new behaviors, learn from the results, and refine their approach based on real-world experience. The module teaches how to increase positive psychological resources, helping clients build optimism, gratitude, self-awareness, and other internal strengths that support sustained behavior change. Students learn to conduct effective final coaching sessions that celebrate progress, consolidate learning, and prepare clients for independent continued growth. The fourth coaching mentor session provides feedback on advanced coaching skills.

### Month 6 — Professional Standards

Month 6 shifts from clinical skills to professional practice. The professional conduct module covers the standards of behavior and communication expected of practicing PHWCs, including client communication protocols, session documentation, and professional presentation. Ethics and legal considerations are explored in depth, covering informed consent, confidentiality obligations, duty to refer, scope of practice boundaries, dual relationships, social media ethics, and the legal frameworks that govern coaching practice in various jurisdictions. Students learn to navigate the gray areas that arise in real-world practice — situations where the right course of action is not immediately obvious and professional judgment must be exercised.

### Month 7 — Health and Wellness Integration

Month 7 broadens the coaching lens to include health content knowledge that supports effective coaching. Health promotion and disease prevention are studied so coaches understand the landscape in which their clients are operating. The chronic disease module provides coaches with enough understanding of common conditions to know when to refer and how to support clients who are managing chronic health challenges alongside their coaching goals. Students learn to assist clients in evaluating and integrating health information from various sources — helping clients become critical consumers of health media rather than passive recipients of conflicting advice. Health behaviors, social determinants of health, and behavioral risk factors are covered to give coaches a population-level understanding of the challenges their clients face.

### Month 8 — Completion and Certification

Month 8 is the culmination of the PHWC program. The practical skills assessment, which is the fifth and final coaching mentor session, evaluates students' coaching competency through a supervised coaching session that is assessed against NBHWC competency standards. The final written exam tests knowledge across all curriculum areas including coaching theory, ethics, health content, and professional practice. Upon successful completion of both the practical assessment and written exam, students graduate with the NTA Certified PHWC credential. Graduation marks the transition from student to practitioner and includes orientation to post-graduation resources including the Practitioner Network, Career Compass, and Nutri-Q access.

## Coaching Tools and Techniques
<!-- source_url: https://www.nutritionaltherapy.com/health-coach -->

### Motivational Interviewing — Core Techniques

Motivational interviewing is the primary communication framework taught in the PHWC program. Originally developed for addiction counseling and subsequently adopted across healthcare and coaching, motivational interviewing is a collaborative conversation style that strengthens a person's own motivation and commitment to change. The approach is built on the recognition that people are more likely to act on decisions they arrive at themselves than on prescriptions from an authority figure. PHWC students learn motivational interviewing as a set of integrated skills that work together to create a non-judgmental, empowering dialogue that helps clients explore their ambivalence about change and find their own reasons to move forward.

### Reflective Listening and Open-Ended Questions

Reflective listening is the practice of listening deeply to a client and then reflecting back what was heard — not as a parrot-like repetition but as a thoughtful restatement that demonstrates understanding and often reveals meaning the client had not fully articulated. Simple reflections restate the content; complex reflections add meaning, connect themes, or highlight emotions underneath the words. Open-ended questions are questions that cannot be answered with yes or no, requiring the client to think, explore, and elaborate. Together, reflective listening and open-ended questions form the backbone of the coaching conversation, keeping the focus on the client's experience and promoting deeper self-exploration than closed or leading questions would allow.

### Affirmations, Reflections, and Summaries

Beyond reflective listening and open-ended questions, motivational interviewing employs affirmations and summaries as key techniques. Affirmations are genuine, specific acknowledgments of client strengths, efforts, or qualities — not generic praise but targeted recognition that builds self-efficacy. Summaries are structured recaps of what has been discussed during a session, serving multiple purposes: they demonstrate that the coach has been listening carefully, they help organize the client's thoughts, and they create natural transition points in the conversation. The combination of open-ended questions, affirmations, reflections, and summaries is sometimes referred to as the OARS framework in motivational interviewing literature and forms the practical skill set that PHWC students practice throughout their training.

### Health and Wellness Wheel Assessment

The Health and Wellness Wheel is a proprietary assessment tool administered through the Nutri-Q software platform. Unlike the NTP's Nutritional Assessment Questionnaire (NAQ), which surveys physiological symptoms, the Health and Wellness Wheel explores multiple dimensions of the client's overall wellness — including physical health, emotional wellbeing, relationships, career satisfaction, financial health, spiritual life, and personal growth. The wheel visualization helps clients identify which areas of their life are most and least satisfying, providing a starting point for goal setting that honors the whole person rather than focusing exclusively on nutrition or physical health. The assessment is readministered periodically to track progress across all wellness dimensions.

### Goal Setting and Action Plan Co-Creation

Goal setting in the PHWC framework is fundamentally collaborative — the coach and client co-create goals rather than the coach prescribing them. PHWCs are trained to help clients set goals that are self-determined, meaningful, specific, and achievable. The process begins with exploring the client's values and vision, then translating that vision into concrete objectives, and finally breaking objectives into actionable steps. Coaches learn to recognize the difference between a goal the client truly owns and a goal the client thinks they should pursue, and to gently explore this distinction when it arises. Action plans are treated as living documents that are refined through ongoing experimentation and reflection rather than fixed commitments.

### Readiness Assessment and Stages of Change

PHWCs are trained to assess client readiness for change, understanding that not every client who seeks coaching is ready to take action. The Transtheoretical Model of Change, which describes the stages of pre-contemplation, contemplation, preparation, action, maintenance, and potential relapse, provides a framework for understanding where a client is in their change process. Coaches learn to match their approach to the client's stage — offering information and awareness for clients in pre-contemplation, exploring ambivalence with those in contemplation, supporting planning for those in preparation, and celebrating and reinforcing for those in action and maintenance. Attempting to push a client to action before they are ready typically produces resistance rather than progress.

## PHWC Program Structure
<!-- source_url: https://www.nutritionaltherapy.com/phwc-student-information -->

### Duration, Format, and Time Commitment

The PHWC program is an 8-month, fully online, instructor-led certification course. Students should expect to dedicate approximately 8 to 10 hours per week to coursework, reading, assignments, and studying, though some program pages cite 10 to 15 hours. The program is designed to be manageable alongside full-time employment. All instruction is delivered through the Canvas learning management system, which houses recorded lectures, discussion forums, required reading assignments, live call Zoom links, replay recordings, and assignment submission portals.

### Live Calls and Canvas Learning Platform

Weekly live calls provide direct interaction with instructors and fellow students. Two time slot options are available per cohort to accommodate different schedules. Live calls include instruction, Q&A, skill demonstration, and practice opportunities. All calls are recorded and available in Canvas for students who cannot attend. The Canvas platform provides a structured learning pathway with module content released progressively throughout the 8-month curriculum, ensuring students build skills in the intended sequence.

### Coach Mentor Sessions — 5 Throughout Program

A distinctive feature of the PHWC program is the five structured coaching mentor sessions distributed across the curriculum. Mentor sessions provide supervised coaching practice where students conduct actual coaching sessions and receive direct feedback from experienced coaches and program faculty. The mentor sessions occur in months 3 (two sessions), 4, 5, and 8 (the final practical skills assessment). This progressive mentorship structure ensures that students receive guidance and correction as their skills develop, building competency and confidence before independent practice. The fifth mentor session serves as the practical skills assessment for graduation.

### Attendance Policy

Students may miss a maximum of 3 live lectures during the 8-month program. Missing more than three live sessions or failing to complete the associated make-up assignments prevents graduation. This policy reflects the program's emphasis on live participation as a core component of coaching skill development — coaching is inherently interactive and cannot be fully learned from passive content consumption alone.

### Cohort Schedule and Enrollment Dates

The PHWC program offers cohort start dates in the spring and fall. The upcoming cohort begins October 5, 2026, with live class times on Tuesdays at 9 AM Pacific Time or Thursdays at 5 PM Pacific Time. Early bird registration offers significant tuition savings. Students select their preferred live call time slot during enrollment.

## PHWC Learning Outcomes
<!-- source_url: https://www.nutritionaltherapy.com/health-coach -->

### Core Competencies at Graduation

Graduates of the PHWC program exit with a defined set of coaching competencies. They can guide meaningful client behavior change using diverse coaching techniques including motivational interviewing and behavior change psychology frameworks. They can foster safe, supportive coaching environments built on trust and non-judgment. They can employ active listening and motivational interviewing for non-judgmental dialogue that empowers client self-discovery. They can assess readiness and co-create action plans aligned with client-determined goals. And they can synthesize diverse health information so clients can make confident, informed choices about their wellness journey.

### NBHWC Board Certification — Requirements Beyond the PHWC Program

The PHWC program provides the approved training hours required by the National Board for Health and Wellness Coaching, but additional requirements must be met independently before sitting for the HWC Certifying Examination. Candidates must complete at least 50 documented coaching sessions — these can include sessions completed during the program and post-graduation. Candidates must also hold either an associate's degree or higher, or have 4,000 hours of documented work experience in any field. The NBHWC exam tests competency across the full range of health and wellness coaching skills. Earning the NBC-HWC credential provides nationally recognized board certification that enhances professional credibility and may open doors to employment in healthcare systems and organizations that require board-certified coaches.

### Career Pathways — Private Practice and Group Coaching

The most common career path for PHWC graduates is private practice, offering one-on-one coaching sessions, group coaching programs, and wellness workshops focused on behavior change and sustainable lifestyle modification. PHWCs can also create online courses, digital memberships, and educational guides that scale their impact beyond individual sessions. The coaching model lends itself well to virtual delivery, allowing practitioners to work with clients regardless of geographic location.

### Career Pathways — Allied Partnerships and Healthcare Settings

PHWC graduates can build careers working alongside licensed healthcare providers, including chiropractors, naturopaths, primary care physicians, and specialists in clinical settings such as hospitals, rehabilitation centers, nursing homes, and veteran affairs facilities. In these settings, the PHWC provides the behavior change support that complements the provider's clinical care. PHWCs can also work within fitness organizations, community centers, and online fitness companies where coaching enhances the exercise experience with holistic wellness support.

### Career Pathways — Corporate Wellness and Education

Corporate wellness represents a growing market for PHWC graduates, encompassing employee wellness programs, workplace workshops, corporate retreats, and organizational speaking engagements. In education, PHWCs can contribute to public school health programs, higher education wellness initiatives, and nonprofit organizations focused on community health advocacy. These pathways allow PHWCs to scale their impact from individual clients to populations and organizations.

## Post-Graduation Support for PHWCs
<!-- source_url: https://www.nutritionaltherapy.com/health-coach -->

### Practitioner Network Membership

PHWC graduates have access to the same Practitioner Network available to NTP graduates. The network provides monthly member-only events, exclusive resources, professional collaboration opportunities, and advanced training webinars. New PHWC graduates receive a free 60-day trial membership. The network creates a community of practice where coaches can continue developing their skills, share experiences, and receive peer support in their professional journey.

### Career Compass

PHWC graduates are eligible for Career Compass, the same 10-week business launch course available to NTP graduates. The course covers legal business structure, branding, service design, client conversion, and marketing — all essential skills for building a sustainable coaching practice. Career Compass includes NTASites website builder access and marketing content templates.

### Nutri-Q Access — PHWC-Specific Features

PHWCs access a version of Nutri-Q tailored to coaching practice. While NTPs use Nutri-Q primarily for the NAQ and Symptom Burden Graph, PHWCs use it for the Health and Wellness Wheel Assessment, goal and motivation tracking, resource and barrier identification, and client progress monitoring. The platform provides detailed questionnaires for intake and ongoing assessment, automated reporting, and HIPAA-compliant record keeping. Students gain access during the program and retain it after graduation.

### Practitioner Directory

PHWC graduates can opt into the NTA's public-facing Practitioner Directory, making themselves discoverable by potential clients seeking health and wellness coaching. The directory listing provides visibility and client referral opportunities alongside NTP and FNTP practitioners listed in the same directory.
