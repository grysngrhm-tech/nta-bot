## NTP Scope of Practice
<!-- source_url: https://www.nutritionaltherapy.com/ntp-scope-of-practice -->

### What Is a Nutritional Therapy Practitioner

A Nutritional Therapy Practitioner (NTP) is a professional educated by the Nutritional Therapy Association, Inc. NTPs are trained to evaluate nutritional needs and provide bio-individualized recommendations based on client interviews, food journal analysis, and nutritional assessment questionnaires. The NTP credential is earned by completing the 12-month NTP certification program and meeting all graduation requirements. NTPs are nutrition professionals capable of providing customized, targeted dietary, supplement, and lifestyle recommendations to support clients in achieving their health goals. Their role centers on reversing preventable poor health through a bio-individualized, food-first approach grounded in the body's innate intelligence and capacity to heal when properly supported.

### What NTPs Are Trained To Do — Assessment and Evaluation

NTPs are trained to conduct comprehensive nutritional assessments using a variety of tools and techniques. They analyze client intake information relative to health goals, reviewing detailed questionnaires, food journals, and lifestyle assessments to develop a complete picture of each client's nutritional status. Specifically, NTPs evaluate intake information to create customized recommendations, assess bio-individual nutritional needs, conduct informative interviews and assessments with clients, evaluate Food and Mood Journals and make recommendations for beneficial dietary changes, and evaluate the Nutritional Assessment Questionnaire (NAQ) through the Nutri-Q software platform to interpret the resulting data. The assessment process is designed to honor bio-individuality by recognizing that each person's nutritional needs are unique and cannot be addressed through a one-size-fits-all approach.

### What NTPs Are Trained To Do — Recommendations and Education

NTPs provide actionable, bio-individualized recommendations across three key areas: diet, supplements, and lifestyle. On the dietary side, NTPs suggest nutrient-dense foods with an emphasis on variety, balance, and traditional preparation methods. They educate clients on how to prepare nutrient-dense foods and incorporate them into daily life, with a focus on food quality, sourcing, and the principles of a properly prepared, nutrient-dense whole food diet. NTPs also suggest individual foundational supplement recommendations based on assessment data, helping clients understand supplementation quality, formulations, efficacy, and appropriate timing. In the lifestyle domain, NTPs provide guidance on key areas such as stress management, sleep hygiene, and intentional movement. All recommendations are rooted in the NTA's food-first philosophy and the Five Foundations of Health framework, which recognizes nutrient-dense diet, digestion, blood sugar regulation, sleep, and stress management as the core pillars of optimal health.

### What NTPs Are Trained To Do — Professional Practice and Referrals

NTPs are trained to operate as collaborative members of a client's health support team. They refer clients to licensed medical professionals when a client requires medical or mental health interventions that fall outside the NTP scope. NTPs can work alongside physicians, chiropractors, naturopaths, and other licensed practitioners either in-office or in private practice to support a doctor's diagnosis and treatment plan. They also educate clients on direct-to-consumer health tools including functional lab testing, wearable devices, and functional assessments. The NTP's professional role is fundamentally educational — providing non-biased health and nutrition information to clients and the broader community. NTPs leverage traditional wisdom, clinical experience, and scientific research in their practice.

### What NTPs Cannot Do — Clinical and Medical Limitations

The NTP scope of practice has clearly defined boundaries around clinical and medical activities. NTPs cannot diagnose, treat, prevent, prescribe, or cure any pathological condition, illness, or disease. They cannot practice Medical Nutrition Therapy (MNT), which is a regulated activity in many jurisdictions. NTPs cannot make unproven health claims about the efficacy of nutritional interventions for specific diseases or conditions. A Nutritional Therapy Practitioner is not a licensed nutritionist or dietitian, and the NTP credential does not confer the legal authority associated with those regulated titles. NTPs must not work with clients who have medical or mental health conditions that require licensed professional intervention without appropriate referral to qualified practitioners.

### What NTPs Cannot Do — Regulatory and Representation Limits

NTPs cannot state or imply that they hold state licensure or certification beyond their NTA credential. They cannot misrepresent their training, title, or qualifications in any way. NTPs must not operate outside their defined scope of practice unless they hold additional national or state licensure in another regulated profession. It is critical that NTPs understand and comply with the regulatory landscape in their specific state or jurisdiction, as recognition and regulatory requirements for nutritional therapy practitioners vary by location. Most states allow independent NTP practice, but some may require additional licensure, supervision, or specific business structures. Practitioners bear responsibility for verifying and complying with their state-specific requirements before beginning practice.

### NTP Philosophical Approach — Food-First, Bio-Individual, Root-Cause

The NTP approach is distinguished by several core philosophical commitments that differentiate it from conventional nutrition advice. The food-first approach prioritizes whole, thoughtfully prepared, nutrient-dense foods as the primary intervention, rather than relying on supplements or restrictive protocols. Bio-individuality is the recognition that each person is biologically unique and that no single dietary approach works for everyone — NTPs tailor their recommendations to each client's individual physiology, health history, and goals. The root-cause focus means NTPs seek to understand why the body is responding a certain way rather than simply addressing surface-level symptoms. NTPs honor the body's innate intelligence — its inherent capacity to restore, regenerate, and correct itself when given the proper nutritional and lifestyle support. The NTA takes an explicitly omnivorous perspective on diet, meaning NTPs are not tied to any single dietary ideology such as veganism, keto, or paleo, but rather assess what works best for each individual client.

### State Regulatory Considerations for NTPs

The regulatory landscape for nutritional therapy practitioners varies significantly across the United States. The NTA advises all graduates to research and understand the specific regulations in their state or jurisdiction before beginning practice. In most states, NTPs can practice independently as nutrition educators and wellness professionals. However, some states have more restrictive regulatory frameworks that may require additional licensure, specific business structures, or supervision by a licensed healthcare provider. The NTA provides resources to help graduates navigate these requirements, but the responsibility for compliance rests with the individual practitioner. The NTA itself is a private vocational school in Washington State, licensed by the Washington State Workforce Training and Education Coordinating Board, and is not accredited by the U.S. Department of Education.

## FNTP Scope of Practice
<!-- source_url: https://www.nutritionaltherapy.com/ntp-scope-of-practice -->

### What Is a Functional Nutritional Therapy Practitioner

A Functional Nutritional Therapy Practitioner (FNTP) is an NTP who has completed additional advanced training through the Functional Clinical Assessment (FCA) course. The FNTP credential builds upon the full NTP scope of practice, adding specialized hands-on assessment capabilities that allow for more detailed and personalized client evaluations. The FNTP designation represents the highest clinical credential offered by the NTA and is earned by completing both the 12-month NTP program and the 9-week FCA course, which includes an in-person workshop component. FNTPs retain all the capabilities and limitations of the NTP scope while gaining additional tools for assessment.

### FNTP Additional Capabilities — Functional Clinical Assessment

The Functional Clinical Assessment is a hands-on technique that allows FNTPs to communicate directly with the body through targeted palpation. FNTPs learn to assess specific reflex points on the body to identify areas of organ stress and dysfunction. This assessment method provides bio-individual feedback that helps practitioners develop more targeted and personalized nutritional recommendations. The FCA process involves systematic palpation of anatomically significant reflex points, interpretation of the body's neurological and physiological responses, and correlation of findings with the client's nutritional assessment data from the NAQ and other intake tools. The FCA is performed in person, which is why the course includes a mandatory three-day in-person workshop where students practice these hands-on techniques under instructor supervision.

### FNTP Additional Capabilities — Lingual-Neural Testing

Lingual-Neural Testing (LNT) is a specialized assessment technique taught exclusively in the FCA course. LNT evaluates the body's neurological response to specific substances placed on the tongue, providing immediate bio-individual feedback about which nutrients or supplements may best support a particular client. This technique allows FNTPs to create highly personalized, validated supplement protocols rather than relying solely on generalized recommendations. LNT works by leveraging the tongue's dense nerve supply and its direct neurological connection to the brain, allowing practitioners to observe real-time physiological responses to nutritional substances. This technique is unique to the FNTP credential and cannot be performed by NTPs who have not completed the FCA course.

### How FNTP Scope Extends Beyond NTP

The FNTP scope encompasses everything within the NTP scope of practice plus the additional capabilities granted by FCA training. The key extensions are the ability to perform hands-on Functional Clinical Assessments using reflex point palpation, and the ability to utilize Lingual-Neural Testing for supplement validation. All other scope boundaries remain identical — FNTPs still cannot diagnose, treat, or prescribe, and they are still bound by the same clinical, medical, and regulatory limitations as NTPs. The FNTP credential represents enhanced assessment capability within the existing NTP framework, not an expansion into medical or clinical territory. FNTPs use these additional tools to gather more detailed data, which feeds into the same NACA (Nutritional Analysis and Clinical Application) process that all NTPs use to develop recommendations.

### FCA Course Requirements and Prerequisites

The Functional Clinical Assessment course is a 9-week program consisting of online coursework followed by a mandatory three-day in-person workshop. The course costs approximately $2,000 and has limited capacity of approximately 22 spots per cohort. The prerequisite is graduation from the NTP program with an active NTP credential — the FCA course is not open to PHWC graduates or individuals who have not completed the NTP certification. The curriculum covers body mechanics, reflex point anatomy, Lingual-Neural Testing techniques, palpation skills, data analysis and interpretation, Coca's Pulse Test, nutrient recommendation development, and professional conduct standards for hands-on assessment. Upon successful completion, graduates earn the FNTP (Functional Nutritional Therapy Practitioner) credential.

## PHWC Scope of Practice
<!-- source_url: https://www.nutritionaltherapy.com/phwc-scope-of-practice -->

### What Is a Professional Health and Wellness Coach

A Professional Health and Wellness Coach (PHWC) is a professional educated by the Nutritional Therapy Association to serve as a facilitator in the behavior change process. PHWCs support clients in discovering their own strength and abilities to achieve their health and wellness goals. The PHWC credential is fundamentally different from the NTP credential in both philosophy and practice — where NTPs are trained to be nutrition experts who make recommendations, PHWCs are trained to be coaching professionals who empower clients to make their own informed choices. The NTA's PHWC program is an Approved Health and Wellness Coach Training and Education Program by the National Board for Health and Wellness Coaching (NBHWC), and graduates may pursue the nationally recognized NBC-HWC certification.

### What PHWCs Are Trained To Do — Coaching Skills and Tools

PHWCs are equipped with a comprehensive set of coaching tools and communication techniques. Their primary tool is motivational interviewing, which encompasses reflective listening, open-ended questions, affirmations, reflections, and summaries. PHWCs are trained to use the Health and Wellness Wheel Assessment through the Nutri-Q software platform to help clients visualize and explore different dimensions of their wellness. They employ coaching presence and frameworks, behavior change psychology, unconditional positive regard, and empathy to create a productive coaching relationship. PHWCs understand the psychology behind behavior change, including readiness assessment and the stages of change model, which allows them to meet clients exactly where they are in their wellness journey.

### What PHWCs Are Trained To Do — Client Partnership and Support

PHWCs partner with clients in several key ways that distinguish their role from that of a nutrition expert. They review intake information to assess a client's readiness for change and support clients in exploring their values and sense of purpose. PHWCs empower clients to design their own action steps toward self-determined goals — the goals always come from the client, not the coach. They partner with clients in designing health visions, setting goals, and creating action steps. PHWCs foster resilience and growth mindset in their clients, supporting habit identification and development. They leverage insights from nationally recognized sources and scientific research to inform their coaching approach. PHWCs can collaborate with a client's existing medical team on doctor-prescribed protocols, and they work in private practice or within healthcare systems. When a client's needs exceed the PHWC scope, coaches refer to licensed professionals.

### What PHWCs Cannot Do — Recommendations and Prescriptions

The most critical limitation of the PHWC scope — and the primary distinction from the NTP scope — is that PHWCs cannot recommend food, supplements, or lifestyle habits for clients to incorporate. This is not merely a semantic distinction; it reflects a fundamentally different approach to client work. While an NTP evaluates assessment data and tells a client what dietary changes, supplements, or lifestyle modifications to make, a PHWC facilitates a process through which the client arrives at their own decisions. PHWCs cannot direct or determine client goals and objectives — the client is always the expert in their own life. This boundary exists because the PHWC role is about empowerment and self-efficacy, not expert prescription.

### What PHWCs Cannot Do — Clinical and Medical Limitations

Like NTPs, PHWCs are bound by strict clinical and medical limitations. A PHWC is not trained to provide medical advice or recommendations. PHWCs may not diagnose, treat, prevent, prescribe, or cure any pathological condition, illness, or disease. They cannot make unproven health claims or misrepresent their training or credentials. PHWCs must not work outside their defined scope unless they hold additional national or state licensure in another regulated profession. These limitations parallel those of the NTP scope but are even broader in one key respect: while NTPs can make nutritional and supplement recommendations, PHWCs cannot make recommendations of any kind — they can only facilitate the client's own decision-making process.

### PHWC Coaching Foundations — Client-Centered, Bio-Individuality, Trust, Resilience, Ethics

The PHWC program is built on five foundational principles that guide every aspect of the coaching relationship. Client-Centered means recognizing the client as the expert in their own life, strengthening their self-confidence and belief in their capacity for progress. Bio-Individuality acknowledges that there is no one-size-fits-all approach to health coaching, and coaches must tailor their methods to meet each individual client where they are. Trust requires coaches to create safe spaces where clients feel seen, heard, encouraged, trusted, and supported through active listening and compassion. Resilience involves fostering growth mindsets and encouraging self-compassion when clients face ambivalence or obstacles on their wellness journey. Ethics requires coaches to prioritize their own health and wellness while maintaining strict professional boundaries and scope limitations, ensuring they never overstep into territory that belongs to licensed professionals or nutrition experts.

### NBHWC Board Certification Pathway

Graduates of the NTA's PHWC program are eligible to pursue the NBC-HWC (National Board Certified Health and Wellness Coach) certification through the National Board for Health and Wellness Coaching. The NBHWC certification is a nationally recognized credential that demonstrates competency in health and wellness coaching. However, PHWC program completion alone does not automatically qualify a graduate for the board exam. Additional requirements beyond the PHWC program include completion of at least 50 documented coaching sessions and either an associate's degree or higher, or 4,000 hours of documented work experience. The NBHWC certification process involves passing the HWC Certifying Examination. The NTA's PHWC program provides the approved training hours required by NBHWC, but graduates must independently meet the additional session and education or experience requirements before sitting for the exam.

## NTP vs PHWC — Critical Distinctions
<!-- source_url: https://www.nutritionaltherapy.com/health-coach -->

### The Core Difference — Recommending vs Empowering

The fundamental distinction between the NTP and PHWC credentials can be summarized in one sentence: an NTP seeks to fulfill the role of a health and nutrition expert, while a PHWC seeks to offer support, encouragement, and reflection that empowers a client to truly own the direction and outcomes of their wellness journey. This is not a difference of degree but of kind. The NTP program focuses on the Foundations of Health, training practitioners to determine food, lifestyle, and supplement recommendations for bio-individual needs. The PHWC program focuses on skills and techniques that help clients achieve sustainable, lasting behavior change. The NTP is data-driven and science-focused, building nutrition expertise. The PHWC is empathy-driven, building coaching psychology skills for behavior change facilitation.

### How NTA Frames the Distinction

The NTA is deliberate and consistent in how it frames the relationship between its two primary credentials. The NTP is positioned as a nutrition professional — someone who can evaluate, analyze, and recommend. The PHWC is positioned as a coaching professional — someone who can listen, support, and empower. The NTA explicitly states that PHWCs do not make recommendations while NTPs do. This distinction is maintained throughout all NTA marketing materials, program descriptions, and scope of practice documents. The NTA views these as complementary but distinct roles within the health and wellness ecosystem, each serving a different client need and requiring a different skill set.

### When to Recommend NTP vs PHWC to a Prospective Student

For NTA employees helping prospective students choose between programs, the key differentiating questions center on the student's career goals and natural orientation. Students who are drawn to the science of nutrition, who want to analyze data and make specific dietary and supplement recommendations, and who are comfortable in an expert advisory role should be directed toward the NTP program. Students who are drawn to coaching psychology, who want to help people achieve lasting behavior change, who prefer to empower rather than prescribe, and who are comfortable with a facilitative rather than directive role should be directed toward the PHWC program. The NTP requires approximately 15-20 hours per week over 12 months and costs approximately $8,000. The PHWC requires approximately 8-10 hours per week over 8 months and costs approximately $5,000. Both programs are fully online and instructor-led with live weekly calls.

### Can Someone Hold Both Credentials

Yes, an individual can hold both the NTP and PHWC credentials. Holding both credentials would allow a practitioner to both make nutrition and supplement recommendations (NTP scope) and provide professional behavior change coaching (PHWC scope). This combination could be particularly powerful for practitioners who want to offer comprehensive client support — using NTP skills to develop personalized nutrition plans and PHWC skills to help clients actually implement and sustain those changes. The NTA does not currently offer a combined or accelerated pathway for earning both credentials; each program must be completed separately with its full curriculum and requirements.
